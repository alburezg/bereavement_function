PART I — DEMOGRAPHIC THEORY
================================================================================

── 1. THE KINSHIP SURFACE (Caswell's matrix model) ──────────────────────────────

The function bereavement() operates downstream of Caswell's formal demographic
kinship model, implemented in R as DemoKin (IvanWilli/DemoKin on GitHub). A
brief account of that model is necessary to understand what bereavement() does.

Caswell (2019, 2021, 2022) formulated kinship dynamics as a stage-classified
matrix population model. For a "focal" individual — a reference person whose kin
we track — the model computes the expected number of living relatives of each
type (parent, grandparent, sibling, child, etc.) at each age of the focal
person. The model is deterministic in expectation and treats kin counts as
continuous-valued (not integer-valued); this is important for understanding the
non-integer exponents in the bereavement formula.

The core objects are:

  U(t): the age-classified survival operator (Leslie matrix without fertility)
        for year t. U[i,j] = probability that a person in age class j-1 survives
        to age class i in year t. For a single-sex model, this is a 101x101
        subdiagonal matrix: U[a+1, a] = p(a, t), the survival probability at age
        a in year t.

  F(t): the age-classified fertility operator. F[0, a] = f(a, t) * s_f, where
        f(a, t) is the age-specific fertility rate and s_f is the sex ratio of
        female births (typically 1/2.04 ~= 0.49).

  L(a, a', k, y): the kinship surface -- the expected number of living relatives
        of relationship type k, aged a', for a focal individual of age a in
        calendar year y. This is the central output of DemoKin::kin() and
        DemoKin::kin2sex().

In the two-sex model (kin2sex), U and F are defined separately for males and
females, and the kinship surface acquires an additional sex-of-kin dimension:

  L(a, a', k, y, g): expected living relatives of type k, aged a', sex g in {f,m},
        for a focal individual of age a in year y.

In the time-varying model (time_invariant = FALSE), the demographic operators
U(t) and F(t) vary with calendar year t, so the kinship counts of a focal
person of age a in year y depend on the entire history of rates from birth year
(y - a) to year y. This is the cohort-period interaction that makes the
time-varying model computationally intensive.

DemoKin's kin2sex() function implements all of this internally and returns a
data frame called kin_full (extracted from the list output as
kin_out$kin_full), with one row per combination of:

  (year, cohort, age_focal, age_kin, sex_kin, sex_focal, kin)

Relevant columns for bereavement():

  year:       calendar year (= cohort + age_focal in time-varying output)
  age_focal:  age of the focal individual (0 to 100)
  age_kin:    age of the relative (0 to 100)
  sex_kin:    sex of the relative ("f" or "m", only in two-sex output)
  sex_focal:  sex of focal individual ("f" or "m")
  kin:        relationship type code (see Table A below)
  living:     expected number of living relatives of this type and age [continuous]
  dead:       expected number of relatives who have died [cumulative; see §4.2]


── Table A: DemoKin kin type codes ──────────────────────────────────────────────

  Code  Relationship             Notes
  ----  -----------------------  -------------------------------------------------
  m     Mother/Father            Single individual; expected count peaks near 1
  d     Child (daughter/son)     Count increases with fertility
  gm    Grandparent              Maternal grandmother by default in 1-sex model
  gd    Grandchild
  ggm   Great-grandparent
  ggd   Great-grandchild
  os    Older sibling            Older than focal at all focal ages
  ys    Younger sibling          Younger than focal; count grows over focal's life
  oa    Older aunt/uncle         Parent's older sibling
  ya    Younger aunt/uncle       Parent's younger sibling
  nos   Niece/nephew (older s.)  Sibling's older child
  nys   Niece/nephew (younger)
  coa   Cousin (older aunt)
  cya   Cousin (younger aunt)

In two-sex output, sex_kin distinguishes male and female relatives within each
code. For example, "m" with sex_kin="f" is the mother; sex_kin="m" is the
father. In practice DemoKin uses "m" for the maternal kin line and automatically
tracks paternal kin via the two-sex algebra.


── 2. THE BEREAVEMENT PROBLEM ───────────────────────────────────────────────────

Given the kinship surface L(a, a', k, y, g), we want to answer:

  "How many people in the population, of age a in year y, lose at least one
   relative of type k during the year y?"

This is a PERIOD bereavement estimate: a cross-sectional quantity for a given
calendar year. It is NOT a life-course (cohort) cumulative measure. We make no
claim about how many times a person has experienced bereavement across their
life; only what fraction of the population experiences it within a single year.

Formally, define:

  q0(a, y, k) = P(focal person of age a in year y loses >=1 relative of type k
                   during year y)

The bereaved count is then:

  B(a, y, k) = q0(a, y, k) x N(a, y)

where N(a, y) is the number of focal individuals of age a alive in year y.

Summing over focal ages gives the total bereaved count:

  B(y, k) = sum_a [ q0(a, y, k) x N(a, y) ]

And the bereaved share of the population:

  b(y, k) = B(y, k) / N(y)    where N(y) = sum_a N(a, y)

The key quantity to compute is q0(a, y, k). Three methods have been proposed
in the literature; only the first is implemented in bereavement().


================================================================================
PART II — THE PRODUCT METHOD
================================================================================

── 3. DERIVATION OF THE PRODUCT FORMULA ─────────────────────────────────────────

The product method (called "Kike's method" in internal project correspondence,
after a collaborator) starts from an independence assumption: the deaths of
different relatives during year y are independent events conditional on their
age and sex. This is an approximation -- in reality, relatives share environments
(infectious disease, conflict, famine) that induce positive correlation among
deaths -- but it provides a clean closed-form estimator and behaves as an upper
bound in the presence of positive death clustering (see §5.2 for the NegBin
alternative that relaxes this assumption).

Under independence, the probability that a specific relative of age a' and sex g
survives year y is:

  P(survives) = 1 - q(a', y, g)

where q(a', y, g) is the age-sex-year-specific probability of dying.

Now, L(a, a', k, y, g) is the expected (continuous) count of living relatives
of type k, aged a', sex g, for a focal person of age a in year y. The key
modelling step is to treat this expected count as if it were the actual count of
independent individuals, each facing probability q(a', y, g) of dying. Under
independence, the probability that all of them survive is:

  P(all survive | kin aged a', sex g) = (1 - q(a', y, g))^{L(a, a', k, y, g)}

The exponent is a continuous real number because L is continuous-valued (from
the matrix model). The expression (1-q)^L is interpreted as exp(L x log(1-q)),
the natural extension of the Binomial survival probability to non-integer counts.
This is equivalent to evaluating the probability generating function (PGF) of a
Poisson distribution with mean L at the point (1-q):

  PGF_{Poisson(L)}(1-q) = exp(L x log(1-q)) = (1-q)^L

This means: if the actual number of relatives at age a' is Poisson(L), the
probability that all of them survive year y is exactly (1-q)^L under
independence. The formula is therefore EXACT under the assumptions of Poisson-
distributed kin counts and independence of deaths, not merely an approximation.

Note: exp(L x log(1-q)) =/= exp(-Lq) unless q is small. The product formula
uses the EXACT form (1-q)^L rather than the Poisson approximation exp(-Lq).
The difference matters when q is large (old ages, high mortality, conflict).

Extending over all ages a' of kin type k and both sexes g, under independence:

  P(all kin of type k survive year y | focal age a) =
    prod_{a'} prod_{g in {f,m}} (1 - q(a', y, g))^{L(a, a', k, y, g)}

where the products run over all ages a' in {0,...,100} and sexes g present in
kin_full. Rows with living = 0 contribute (1-q)^0 = 1 and drop out automatically.

Therefore:

  q0(a, y, k) = 1 - prod_{a', g} (1 - q(a', y, g))^{L(a, a', k, y, g)}   [Eq. 1]

This is the core formula of bereavement(). In code (Steps 3-4 of the function):

  # Step 3: per-cell contribution (one cell = one age_kin x sex_kin combination)
  p0_cell = (1 - qx_kin)^living

  # Step 4: product within each (year, age_focal, kin) group
  p0 = prod(p0_cell)

  # q0 = 1 - p0 is computed implicitly in Step 6:
  bereaved = (1 - p0) x pop_focal


── 4. YEAR ALIGNMENT AND THE 'living' vs. 'dead' COLUMNS ───────────────────────

4.1  Which column to use

kin_full contains both 'living' and 'dead' columns. The product method uses
'living', not 'dead'. This is because:

  - 'living' gives the expected kin alive at the START of year y for a focal
    person of age a. Combined with q(a', y, g), this gives the expected deaths
    during year y directly from Eq. 1.

  - 'dead' in DemoKin output is a CUMULATIVE count: total deaths of kin type k
    from the focal person's birth up to year y. It is NOT the period deaths
    occurring specifically during year y.

The product method therefore avoids any differencing of the dead column across
consecutive years, which is what the Poisson method (§5.1) must do, and which
requires kin_full for two consecutive years.

4.2  Year alignment

For a period estimate in year y:
  - kin_full must be filtered to rows where year == y
  - The living counts represent kin alive during year y
  - q(a', y, g) must also refer to year y

These must match: kin_full year = qx year = y. No offset is needed. This is
in contrast to the Poisson method (§5.1), which uses dead counts from year y+1
to approximate period deaths in year y (an intentional one-year offset arising
from the cumulative nature of the dead column in DemoKin).


── 5. ALTERNATIVE METHODS (context only; NOT implemented in bereavement()) ───────

5.1  Poisson / Binomial method (Caswell, Margolis & Verdery)

Uses the cumulative 'dead' column from kin_full, differenced across years to
get approximate period deaths:

  dead_period(a, y, k) = sum_{a'} [dead(a, a', k, y+1) - dead(a, a', k, y)]

(This is why the Gaza pipeline uses year y+1 dead counts for year y bereavement.)

For unconstrained kin (children, siblings, nieces...):
  P(>=1 dies) = 1 - exp(-dead_period)    [Poisson: k -> 0 limit]

For kin with a known fixed group size n (parents: n=2, grandparents: n=4):
  P(>=1 dies) = 1 - (1 - dead_period/n)^n    [Binomial]

Key differences from the product method:
  - Uses DEAD counts (differenced) rather than LIVING counts + qx
  - Requires kin_full for two consecutive years, not just year y
  - Pools all kin ages into a single dead_period scalar before computing P,
    losing the age distribution of kin
  - The product method preserves the full age distribution and applies
    age-specific qx, making it more appropriate for cause-of-death analysis
    where cause-specific qx varies strongly by age

5.2  Negative Binomial method (Moretti; Alburez-Gutierrez et al. 2024)

Relaxes the independence assumption by modelling unobserved frailty
heterogeneity: families with high baseline mortality risk lose multiple members,
so deaths cluster within families. If kin deaths follow a Negative Binomial
distribution with mean mu = dead_period and overdispersion parameter k (the
variance of the Gamma frailty distribution), then:

  P(>=1 dies) = 1 - (1 + k x dead_period)^{-1/k}

As k -> 0: (1 + k x dead_period)^{-1/k} -> exp(-dead_period), recovering Poisson.
As k -> inf: (1 + k x dead_period)^{-1/k} -> 1, meaning everyone is bereaved.

The NegBin arises as a Poisson-Gamma mixture: if deaths ~ Poisson(lambda x theta)
where theta ~ Gamma(mean=1, variance=k), then k is the frailty variance.
A larger k means more death clustering within kin networks.

Empirical calibration (Alburez-Gutierrez et al. 2024, Sci. Adv. 10, eado6951):
  OCHA data showed 61% of Gaza deaths (Oct-Nov 2023) concentrated in 79% of
  multi-fatality families. Gamma frailty simulations recovered:
    k ~  7 for simulated families of size 20
    k ~ 12 for simulated families of size 40
    k ~ 28 for simulated families of size 60
  Since kin groups in the study are much smaller (parents n~2, siblings n~4-6),
  the calibrated k was adjusted proportionally:
    k = 0.5 -> near-Poisson clustering (parents, n~2)
    k = 2.0 -> moderate clustering     (siblings/children, n~4-6)
    k = 5.0 -> strong clustering        (extended kin, n~10-15)

Relation to the product method:
  The product method implicitly assumes k = 0 (independent deaths). When k > 0,
  the NegBin gives FEWER bereaved persons for large kin groups (clustering
  concentrates multiple losses in fewer families, so fewer focal persons lose
  anyone). For small kin groups (parents, n~2), the difference is negligible.
  The product method therefore provides an upper bound on bereavement count
  in populations where death clustering is present (conflict, epidemic, famine).

5.3  Combining q0 across kin types (Nuclear / Extended groupings)

When computing "P(lose any member of a nuclear family in year y | focal age a)",
the union-probability formula applies:

  q0_group(a, y) = 1 - prod_{k in group} (1 - q0(a, y, k))

This uses inclusion-exclusion under the assumption that losses of different kin
types are independent (approximate). In the Gaza sensitivity script this is:

  summarise(q0 = 1 - prod(1 - q0), .by = c(year, age_focal, method, kin_group))

This combination must be performed AFTER computing per-kin-type q0 values and
is NOT inside bereavement(). The caller does it from the returned output.


================================================================================
PART III — PERIOD vs. COHORT INTERPRETATION
================================================================================

── 6. PERIOD PERSPECTIVE (what this function computes) ──────────────────────────

The function computes PERIOD bereavement estimates. In year y, all people aged
a who are currently alive (across all birth cohorts born in year y-a) are treated
as if they had the same kinship surface -- the period kinship surface for year y.
DemoKin supports this by allowing output_period in kin2sex() calls.

Period bereavement in year y for kin type k:
  B(y, k) = sum_a [ q0(a, y, k) x N(a, y) ]

Interpretation: "In year y, B(y, k) people across all ages lose at least one
relative of type k." This is the natural quantity for the cross-sectional social
burden of bereavement in a given calendar year.

── 7. COHORT PERSPECTIVE (not implemented; for context) ─────────────────────────

A cohort bereavement estimate follows people born in year c and asks: "By age a,
what fraction have ever lost a relative of type k?" This requires accumulating
q0 over time while accounting for survivor selection among focal individuals.
DemoKin supports cohort output via output_cohort, but bereavement() is not
designed for this. A cohort version would need to: (a) use cohort-specific
kin_full rows (identified by cohort column, not year column); (b) compute
cumulative P(at least one loss by age a) = 1 - prod_{t=0}^{a} (1 - q0(t, c+t, k));
(c) apply a different population denominator (cohort size at birth, not period
population). This is a substantively distinct function.


================================================================================
PART IV — THE TWO-SEX EXTENSION
================================================================================

── 8. TWO-SEX KINSHIP AND SEX-SPECIFIC MORTALITY ────────────────────────────────

In the two-sex model (kin2sex output), kin_full has a sex_kin column with values
"f" (female kin) and "m" (male kin). The product formula extends naturally:

  q0(a, y, k) = 1 - prod_{a'} prod_{g in {f,m}} (1 - q(a', y, g))^{L(a, a', k, y, g)}

This requires sex-specific mortality: q_f(a', y) for female kin and q_m(a', y)
for male kin. Male mortality is systematically higher than female mortality at
most ages in most populations (male excess mortality); failing to distinguish
them would underestimate bereavement through male relatives and overestimate it
through female relatives.

In bereavement(), two-sex mode is auto-detected:

  two_sex = ("sex_kin" %in% names(kin_full)) AND (n_distinct(kin_full$sex_kin) > 1)

When two_sex = TRUE, the function requires qx to be sex-specific. The pop
(focal population) does NOT need to be sex-specific in two-sex mode. The reason:
all focal individuals in a given kin2sex run share the same sex_focal (e.g., "f").
The pop argument tells us how many focal women of age a are alive in year y.
Passing total population (male + female) as pop is a valid approximation when the
goal is a population-wide bereaved count, as it applies the female-focal q0 to
all individuals. This is noted explicitly in the demo script comments.

The two-sex product (for a given focal age a, year y, kin type k) iterates over
all rows in kin_full that match (age_focal=a, year=y, kin=k). Each row has a
specific (age_kin=a', sex_kin=g) combination, and the function joins on (year,
age_kin, sex_kin) to retrieve the sex-specific qx_kin. This is Step 2 of the
function. The product in Step 4 then automatically covers all (a', g) pairs.


================================================================================
PART V — COMPUTATIONAL ARCHITECTURE
================================================================================

── 9. STEP-BY-STEP COMPUTATION IN bereavement() ─────────────────────────────────

The function has six numbered steps plus two internal helpers. Here is a precise
account of each.

STEP 0: Input validation and mode detection
  - Checks that kin_full has required columns: kin, age_kin, age_focal, year, living
  - Detects two-sex mode: two_sex = ("sex_kin" %in% names(kin_full)) AND
    n_distinct(kin_full$sex_kin) > 1
  - Filters kin_full to output_year if specified. Error if no rows remain.

STEP 1: Normalise qx and pop to canonical long form
  Both qx and pop accept three input formats (handled by internal helper .to_long()):

    a) Matrix:     rows = ages 0-100 (integer rownames), cols = years (integer colnames)
                   -> pivoted to long form: (age, year, value)

    b) Data frame: must have columns year, age, and the value column (qx or pop)
                   -> already in long form; column types coerced to integer

    c) Named list: list(f=<matrix or df>, m=<matrix or df>)
                   -> each element processed by .to_long() and bound with a sex column

  Helper .parse_input() calls .to_long() and enforces:
    - If two_sex=TRUE and require_sex=TRUE (for qx): must have a sex column
    - If two_sex=FALSE: must NOT have multiple sex values
  The require_sex flag is FALSE for pop (sex column optional even in two-sex mode).

STEP 2: Join qx onto kin_full
  The canonical long qx is renamed: age -> age_kin, qx -> qx_kin, sex -> sex_kin.
  A left join is performed:

    kin_full LEFT JOIN qx ON (year, age_kin)              [one-sex mode]
    kin_full LEFT JOIN qx ON (year, age_kin, sex_kin)     [two-sex mode]

  After joining, any NA in qx_kin means qx did not cover some (age, year, sex)
  combination present in kin_full -> stop with informative message.

  Also check for the terminal age problem: if qx_kin == 1 AND living > 0 in any
  row -> stop (see §10 for explanation and fix).

STEP 3: Per-cell survival probability (the innermost factor of Eq. 1)
  For each row in kin_work (one row = one combination of age_focal, age_kin,
  kin type, year, and optionally sex_kin):

    p0_cell = (1 - qx_kin)^living

  When living = 0: p0_cell = 1^0... wait, (1-q)^0 = 1 regardless of q. Correct.
  When living > 0 and qx_kin > 0: p0_cell < 1 (mortality reduces survival prob).
  When qx_kin = 0 (certain survival): p0_cell = 1 regardless of living. Correct.

STEP 4: Product over all kin ages (and sexes) per (year, age_focal, kin)
  Groups: c("year", "age_focal", "kin"), plus "sex_focal" if present in kin_work.
  Within each group:

    p0 = prod(p0_cell)

  This is the probability that ALL relatives of type k survive year y for a focal
  person of age a. The product runs over all rows in the group, i.e., all
  (age_kin, sex_kin) combinations for that kin type. This corresponds exactly to
  the double product in Eq. 1.

STEP 5: Join focal population
  pop_long is renamed: age -> age_focal, pop -> pop_focal, sex -> sex_focal.
  Join keys:
    (year, age_focal)             [if no sex column in pop_join]
    (year, age_focal, sex_focal)  [if sex column present]

  After joining, each row of p0 has a pop_focal value = N(a, y).

STEP 6: Compute bereaved and bereaved_prop
  Total population per year (denominator):
    pop_total = sum(pop_focal) grouped by year = N(y)

  Then:
    bereaved      = (1 - p0) x pop_focal    = q0(a,y,k) x N(a,y)
    bereaved_prop = bereaved / pop_total     = q0(a,y,k) x N(a,y) / N(y)

  Note: bereaved_prop is a per-age-per-kin quantity. Summing bereaved_prop
  over age_focal (for a fixed year and kin type) gives the total share of the
  population bereaved for that kin type. This works because:

    sum_a bereaved_prop(a,y,k) = sum_a [q0(a,y,k) x N(a,y)] / N(y)
                                = B(y,k) / N(y)   = b(y,k)

  Output is a tidy data frame sorted by (year, kin, age_focal) with columns:
    year, age_focal, kin, bereaved, bereaved_prop


── 10. THE TERMINAL AGE PROBLEM ─────────────────────────────────────────────────

Most life tables assign qx = 1 at the open age group (OAG, typically age 100+),
meaning everyone in this age class is assumed to die within the year. In DemoKin's
Swedish data, swe_px[age=100, year] = 0 exactly, so 1 - swe_px[100,] = 1.

If qx_kin = 1 for any age where living > 0, then:

  p0_cell = (1 - 1)^living = 0^living = 0   [for any living > 0]

and the product p0 collapses to 0 regardless of all other kin ages. This makes:

  q0(a, y, k) = 1 - 0 = 1  for EVERY focal age a with any kin of age 100+

i.e., every focal person is predicted to lose kin of type k, which is wrong.

The correct fix is to replace qx at the terminal age with a continuous-time
approximation. If mx is the force of mortality (hazard rate) in the OAG, then
under a constant force of mortality model:

  qx_terminal = 1 - exp(-mx_terminal)

When only px is available and px_terminal > 0: mx = -log(px), so
qx = 1 - exp(-(-log(px))) = 1 - px. This is circular and fails when px = 0.
When px_terminal = 0, we need mx from an external source.

Practical solutions:

  a) Quick fix (demo use only): replace px = 0 with 1e-6 before computing qx.
     qx = 1 - pmax(px, 1e-6)  ->  qx <= 1 - 1e-6 < 1. Avoids the error.
     Justification: at age 100+, the typical annual mortality rate is on the order
     of 50% or more, so qx ~ 0.5-0.8. The 1e-6 replacement gives qx ~ 0.999999,
     which overestimates mortality at age 100 but affects bereavement estimates
     very little because living kin at age 100 is very close to 0 in any
     realistic scenario. Used in demo_bereavement_demokin.R.

  b) Formal fix (for analysis): use DemoTools::lt_single_mx(nMx, Age, Sex, OAG=FALSE)
     to convert mortality rates mx -> full life table nqx via the Reed-Merrell
     method or HMD interpolation. Then override the last age: px[101] = exp(-mx[101]).
     This is the approach used in Gaza pipeline scripts 1-8.

The bereavement() function does NOT silently fix qx = 1 internally. It stops
with an informative error explaining the problem and fix. This is intentional:
silent capping would mask data quality issues in formal analyses.


── 11. INTERNAL HELPERS ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

.to_long(x, value_col, sex_label = NULL)
  Converts a single object (matrix or data frame) to a long data frame with
  columns (age [integer], year [integer], [sex], value_col). For matrices, numeric
  dimnames are required (used as age and year identifiers). If sex_label is non-NULL
  and the result has no sex column, a constant sex column is added (used when calling
  for a single sex within .parse_input).

.parse_input(x, value_col, two_sex, require_sex = TRUE)
  Dispatches on the type of x:
    - Named list(f=..., m=...): calls .to_long() on each, adds sex="f"/"m", binds rows
    - Matrix or data frame: calls .to_long() directly
  Validates sex column presence against two_sex and require_sex flags.
  Returns a canonical long data frame.

These helpers are not exported. They exist to allow bereavement() to accept
multiple input formats without cluttering the main function body.

PART VI — INPUT AND OUTPUT SPECIFICATION
================================================================================

── 12. FUNCTION SIGNATURE ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

bereavement(
  kin_full,
  qx,
  pop,
  output_year = NULL
)


── 13. kin_full ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Source: kin_out$kin_full from DemoKin::kin() or DemoKin::kin2sex().

Required columns:
  kin        character  relationship type code (Table A above)
  age_kin    integer    age of the relative (0-100)
  age_focal  integer    age of the focal individual (0-100)
  year       integer    calendar year
  living     numeric    expected number of living relatives [continuous, >= 0]

Optional columns (auto-detected):
  sex_kin    character  sex of relative ("f", "m") -- triggers two-sex mode
  sex_focal  character  sex of focal ("f", "m")    -- used as grouping variable if present
  cohort     integer    ignored; passed through if present
  dead       numeric    ignored by the product method

In a time-invariant single-sex model (kin() output), the year column may have
a single value or represent the age of the focal; output_year=NULL processes
whatever years are present.

In a time-varying two-sex model (kin2sex() with time_invariant=FALSE), kin_full
can be very large (tens of millions of rows for the full 1900-2018 Swedish data).
Use output_year to restrict to specific years before passing to bereavement().


── 14. qx (and pop, same format rules) ──────────────────────────────────────────

Accepted formats:

  a) Matrix (single sex or sex-agnostic):
     - dim: (101 ages) x (n_years)
     - rownames: character or integer ages 0-100
     - colnames: character or integer years matching those in kin_full

  b) Long data frame, single sex:
     - Required columns: year (integer), age (integer), qx (numeric)

  c) Long data frame, two-sex:
     - Required columns: year (integer), age (integer), sex ("f"/"m"), qx (numeric)

  d) Named list of two-sex matrices:
     - list(f = <101 x n matrix>, m = <101 x n matrix>)
     - Each element in format (a)

Values: probabilities in [0, 1). Must NOT include qx = 1 at ages where living > 0
(see §10). Supply qx for all ages 0-100 and all years in kin_full; any gap
produces an NA and a stop error.

All-cause vs. cause-specific: qx can be all-cause or cause-specific. When
cause-specific, q0(a, y, k) is the probability of losing kin to that specific
cause. This is one advantage of the product method over Poisson/NegBin: those
methods use the 'dead' column in kin_full which is always all-cause.


── 15. pop ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Same format as qx but the value column is named "pop". Sex column is optional
even in two-sex mode (require_sex = FALSE). Values are non-negative real numbers
(population counts).

The pop argument affects only the scale-up steps (5-6). Setting pop to a matrix
of all 1s gives bereaved = q0 and bereaved_prop = q0 / n_ages, which is useful
for inspecting the raw probability surface without a specific population.


── 16. output_year ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

NULL:             compute for all years present in kin_full
Integer vector:   compute only for those years

Error if any requested year is absent from kin_full. The function does not
interpolate or extrapolate the kinship surface; the caller must ensure kin_full
covers the requested years.


── 17. OUTPUT DATA FRAME ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

One row per (year, age_focal, kin). Sorted by (year, kin, age_focal).

  year          integer   calendar year
  age_focal     integer   age of focal individual (0-100)
  kin           character relationship type code
  bereaved      numeric   q0(a,y,k) x N(a,y) = expected bereaved at focal age a
  bereaved_prop numeric   bereaved / N(y) = per-capita contribution to annual rate

To aggregate:
  Total bereaved per kin type per year:  summarise(bereaved = sum(bereaved), .by=c(year,kin))
  Total bereaved share per kin type:     summarise(bereaved_prop = sum(bereaved_prop), .by=c(year,kin))
  Nuclear family union:                  1 - prod(1 - q0) per (year, age_focal), computed externally

sex_focal is retained as an additional column when present in kin_full.

PART VII — ERROR HANDLING DESIGN
================================================================================

── 18. INTENTIONAL ERRORS ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The function fails loudly with descriptive messages rather than silently wrong
results. Bereavement analysis is sensitive to input quality; a quietly capped
qx or silently dropped NA would produce plausible-looking but incorrect numbers.

Error conditions and their rationale:

  1. Missing required columns in kin_full
     -> stop("kin_full is missing required columns: ...")

  2. output_year not found in kin_full$year
     -> stop("output_year = X not found in kin_full$year...")
     Rationale: kin_full cannot be extended inside the function; the caller
     must supply the correct kin2sex output for those years.

  3. NA values in qx after joining
     -> stop("N NA values in qx after joining onto kin_full...")
     Most common cause: qx matrix covers fewer years or ages than kin_full.

  4. qx = 1 with living > 0 (terminal age problem)
     -> stop("qx = 1 detected at age(s) X with living kin > 0...")
     Includes explanation of the collapse mechanism and the fix.
     Does NOT silently cap; caller must fix before calling.

  5. qx not sex-specific in two-sex mode
     -> stop("kin_full has two sexes but qx has no 'sex' column...")

  6. qx is sex-specific in one-sex mode
     -> stop("kin_full is single-sex but qx contains multiple sexes...")

  7. Format errors (missing dimnames, wrong list structure, etc.)
     -> stop() from .to_long() or .parse_input() with informative message

── 21. DESIGN PHILOSOPHY ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The function does exactly one thing: apply the product formula to (kin_full, qx,
pop) and return bereaved counts. It does not run kin2sex(), does not compute
life tables, does not produce plots, and has no side effects. This minimal scope
makes the function composable (run inside sensitivity loops with different qx),
testable (the formula is trivially verifiable), and fast (no redundant computation).

The flexible input formats (matrix / long df / named list) serve two use cases:
  1. New users: matrices with dimnames are the most natural format when working
     directly with DemoKin built-in data or HMD files.
  2. Pipeline users: long data frames in tidyverse style, already prepared from
     larger demographic data workflows.

The strict error handling (no silent failures) reflects the context: bereavement
estimates are used in policy-relevant demographic analyses where silently wrong
numbers are more harmful than an explicit stop() that forces the user to inspect
their inputs.
